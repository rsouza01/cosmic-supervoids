# Project Guide: Measuring the Cosmic Void Radial Density Profile

This project replicates a foundational calculation in observational cosmology: measuring the matter underdensity profile of a cosmic void. By using a public, pre-computed void catalog derived from spectroscopic galaxy redshift surveys, you will verify how the matter density behaves from the deep, underdense core of a supervoid, across its boundary, and into the surrounding compensating shell of the cosmic web.

---

## 1. Theoretical Framework

Cosmic voids are large, low-density regions that dominate the volume of the universe. In the context of cosmic evolution, voids expand faster than the background universe, acting as dominant drivers of the large-scale structure (LSS) geometry.

### The Density Contrast Matrix

To quantify the matter distribution within and around a void, we define the dimensionless density contrast field, $\delta(\mathbf{x})$, as:

$$\delta(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\rho(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{\rho}}{\bar{\rho}}$$

where $\rho(\mathbf{x})$ is the local matter density and $\bar{\rho}$ is the mean background density of the universe.

* In the center of a mature supervoid, $\delta \rightarrow -1.0$ (a total vacuum).
* In practice, due to bias in how galaxies trace dark matter, the galaxy density contrast $\delta_g$ typically reaches a minimum of $\delta_g \sim -0.8$ at the core.

### The Universal Void Profile

Analytical models (such as the shepherding of matter via the excursion set formalism) and $N$-body simulations show that voids exhibit a self-similar, spherically averaged radial profile. A widely adopted empirical fit is the **HSWS profile** (Hamaus, Sutter, Wandelt, & Weinberg, 2014), which parameterizes the profile as:

$$\delta(r) = \delta_0 \frac{1 - (r/r_s)^\alpha}{1 + (r/r_v)^\beta}$$

where:

* $\delta_0$ is the central density contrast.
* $r_s$ and $r_v$ are scale parameters related to the void radius.
* $\alpha$ and $\beta$ govern the slope of the inner wall and the outer descent back to the mean density.

## 3. Data Requirements

To complete this exercise without dealing with the complex selection functions of raw data, you need a pre-computed catalog that contains:

1. **The Target Void Catalog:** A file containing the metadata for each identified void. Each entry must provide the 3D co-moving coordinates of the void center $(X_c, Y_c, Z_c)$ and its effective spherical radius ($R_v$).
2. **The Associated Galaxy Catalog:** The underlying galaxy distribution used by the algorithm, providing the 3D coordinates $(X_g, Y_g, Z_g)$ of every tracer galaxy in that volume.
3. **The Random Catalog:** A synthetic catalog generated by the survey team that mimics the exact angular mask, footprint, and redshift selection function ($\text{d}N/\text{d}z$) of the real galaxy catalog, but filled with uniformly distributed random points.

*Recommended Source:* The **Sutter et al. Void Catalogs** or the **VAST (Void Analysis Software Toolkit)** public data releases based on SDSS DR7 or DR12.

## 4. Step-by-Step Execution Guide

### Step 1: Coordinate System Homogenization

Ensure that both your galaxy catalog, random catalog, and void center coordinates are in the same spatial metric.

* If the catalog provides Right Ascension ($\alpha$), Declination ($\delta$), and Redshift ($z$), you must convert them to co-moving Cartesian coordinates $(X, Y, Z)$ using a standard flat $\Lambda\text{CDM}$ cosmology (typically Planck parameters).
* If using a pre-processed Value-Added Catalog (VAC), verify whether the units are in comoving $\text{Mpc}$ or $\text{h}^{-1}\text{Mpc}$.

### Step 2: Void Selection & Filtering

Large-scale structure catalogs contain a mix of tiny, spurious underdensities and massive cosmic structures.

* Filter your void catalog to isolate **large, well-defined supervoids**. A safe starting threshold is selecting voids with an effective radius $R_v \ge 30 \text{ h}^{-1}\text{Mpc}$.
* Ensure the selected voids are deep enough within the survey boundaries to avoid edge-effect artifacts (this information is often flagged in the catalog as a "distance-to-edge" or "edge-void" boolean).

### Step 3: Concentric Shell Binning (The Single Void Approach)

Choose a single isolated supervoid from your filtered list to study first.

1. Define a set of concentric, spherical shells centered on $(X_c, Y_c, Z_c)$.
2. Instead of using absolute distances (like $10, 20, 30 \text{ Mpc}$), normalize your bin boundaries in units of the void's own effective radius:

$$\lambda = \frac{r}{R_v}$$


3. Establish your bins from $\lambda = 0.0$ (the center) out to $\lambda = 3.0$ (well into the unperturbed cosmic web). A bin width of $\Delta\lambda = 0.1$ works well.

### Step 4: Normalization via the Random Catalog

Because galaxy surveys suffer from selection effects (e.g., fewer galaxies are observed at high redshifts due to flux limits), you cannot simply divide the number of galaxies by the geometric volume of the shell. You must use the Random Catalog to correct for this.

For each shell bin $[\lambda_i, \lambda_{i+1}]$:

1. Count the number of real galaxies falling within the shell: $N_{\text{gal}}(\lambda)$.
2. Count the number of random points falling within the exact same shell: $N_{\text{rand}}(\lambda)$.
3. Compute the density contrast using the ratio of data to randoms, scaled by the total number of particles in the entire survey volume:

$$\delta(\lambda) = \frac{N_{\text{gal}}(\lambda)}{N_{\text{rand}}(\lambda)} \times \frac{N_{\text{rand, total}}}{N_{\text{gal, total}}} - 1$$

### Step 5: Stacking Voids for Signal-to-Noise Enhancement

If you plot $\delta(\lambda)$ for a single void, you will notice extreme fluctuations and high shot noise due to the scarcity of galaxies near the core. To extract the true underlying physical signal, you must stack them.

1. Loop over all your filtered supervoids.
2. For each void, compute the normalized distance $\lambda_j = r_j / R_v$ for every galaxy and random point in its vicinity.
3. Pool all normalized distances together into a single global histogram for galaxies and a single global histogram for randoms.
4. Apply the density contrast equation from Step 4 to the pooled histograms.

---

## 5. Expected Verification Milestones

When you plot your final stacked profile ($\delta(\lambda)$ on the y-axis vs. $\lambda = r/R_v$ on the x-axis), check for these physical features to verify your calculation:

* **The Core ($\lambda \to 0$):** The curve should drop to a deep minimum between $-0.7$ and $-0.9$. It should flatline near the center, representing the empty interior.
* **The Crossing Point ($\lambda \approx 0.8 - 1.0$):** The profile should rise steeply, crossing the $\delta = 0$ line (the average density of the universe) right around the effective radius of the void.
* **The Compensating Ridge ($\lambda \approx 1.0 - 1.3$):** The profile should overshoot $\delta = 0$ and reach a positive peak ($\delta > 0$). This is the structural "shell" or wall of galaxies and filaments that borders the void, conserving matter that was evacuated from the center.
* **Asymptotic Convergence ($\lambda > 2.0$):** As you move far outside the structure, the profile should asymptotically decay back toward $\delta = 0$, indicating that you have reached the unperturbed, homogeneous background of the universe.
